In 1982, Richard Feynman posited that a classical computer could not effectively model the functions of a quantum computer. This lead to the hypothesis that quantum mechanics could be utilised to speed up and make computing more efficient.[@feynman_simulating_1982]

In Steane's 1998 review, he characterises a quantum computer as a theoretical construct, like a thought experiment, whose purpose is to allow quantum information processing to be formally analysed. In the last 25 years, innovation in this sector has evolved it from a theoretical construct into technology that is not only used to analyse the form and method of quantum information processing, but has real-world applications inter alia in Science, Aviation and Cryptography.

## A definition

A definition helps determine the context of the debate. I define quantum computing as most importantly a _computer_ that exploits quantum-mechanics for the purpose of performing operations on data. This definition is key to my key hypothesis the [[Accelerator Thesis on Privacy and Quantum Computing]]

For the physicist, the following equation represents 'universal computer'.[@steane_1998]
$$U (d[T ], x) = T (x)$$

If we provide U with an input tape containing both a description of machine $T$ and the input $x$, then $U$ will compute the same function as $T$ would have done, for any machine $T$ , without an exponential slow-down.

The key principles of quantum mechanics that make quantum computing possible are qubits, superposition and entanglement.
