## Art. 8

### OECD
  -  Profits from the operation of ships shall be taxable in the state of residence.
  
  - The Commentary subsequently states that profits obtained from leasing a ship on charter *fully equipped* must be treated as the profits from the carriage of cargo.
  - When the lease on a bare-boat charter basis, (BBCB or BBCD) then Art. 7 applies.
 - Though where it is an ancillary activity of the enterprise, then again Art. 8 and not Art. 7 shall apply. 
 - Infact, as Vogel notes, India has continuously reserved the right to tax income under Art. 12 and not Art. 8 in respect of the income obtained on a bare-charter basis.  
 
### Singapore Treaty
- The profits derived from the transportation of goods carried on by the lessees or charters shall be profits from the operation of the ship.
- It defines the what constitutes the phrase "profits from the operation of ships."
- International Traffic
  - There is a difference between how the phrase is defined in the OECD MTC and the DTAA.
  - The last line in the MTC states "and the enterprise that operates the ship or aircraft is not an enterprise of that State"
    - It means that even if the boat is operating in the domestic waters of the other State , as long as the operator is not a resident of such State, it constitutes international traffic
  - This section of the MTC has been consciously omitted, such that if the vessel is operated solely between places in the "other Contracting State" then it shall not constitute "international traffic"
  
### Nature of Art. 8 - Vogel et al.
- The priority of Art. 8 only extends to pfofits form the operation of ships in intenrnatinal traffic,. Income from the operation of ships *other than international trafffic falls under Art. 7*. if such income constitutes business profits, such as business profits, fishing income, income from crewing.

- A ship or aircraft is operated solely between places in the other Contracting State in relation to a particular voyage if the place of departure and the place of arrival of the ship or aircraft are both in that other Contracting State. In particular, it is unnecessary for a border to be crossed after every take-off or sailing. Even if a particular voyage includes a series of stopping points within one State, such transportation continues to fall within the scope of operating ships or aircraft in international traffic *unless the ship or aircraft's initial departure and final destination are both in that State.*
- The applicability of Art. 8 is irrespective of whether the ships are owned leased or otherwise placed at the disposal of the enterprise.
- "The same is true for the leasing of a ship or aircraft on charter fully equipped, manned and supplied (also generally referred to as a ‘time charter-party’ or ‘voyage charter-party’ in the shipping industry and as a ‘wet lease’ in the airline industry), which is considered a form of operating ships or aircraft, and profits earned by the lessor are consequently covered by Article 8 OECD and UN MC" -- Para 21 [@beckerKlausVogelDouble2022a]
  - Situation (3). Since S is a time charterer of vessels its profits from the operation of the vessels are considered shipping profits and S is entitled to exclude the income from such operations under section 883 of the Code. It is immaterial for the exclusion to apply whether S charters the vessels directly or through agents. [^1]
  
  - If a German corporate entity leases out crewed aircraft to Austrian aviation companies through the intermediary of its Austrian permanent establishment, then both the German aircraft owner and the Austrian charterer may derive income from the operation of "an enterprise ... of air transport" within the meaning of Article 6 of the 1954 Germany-Austria Double Taxation Agreement.[^2]

## Gosalia Shipping
- There is a difference between the contract for the carriage of goods, and a time-charter for a vessl. If the charterer is liable to pay the charter-party, a fee irrespective of whether vessel is used to carry goods or not, such sum would not be on account of carriage of goods

- A time charter is a contract where *the ownership and posession of the ship remain with the owner, but the control over the vessel's working and navigation are transferred for the time being to the persons who use it.*
- Thus the claim of the revenue under Section 172 of the Act was negativised.

## Poompuhar
- The amount paid by the charterer for the use of the ship constitutes the expression "use or right to use" as found in Clause (iva) in Section 9(1)(vi) Exp. 2

- Royalty in India is modelled after the UN Model as against the updated OECD Model which was amended to include  "industrial commercial, scientific experience" replacing the previous word "equipment" though the UN Model and the Indian Statute (and its DTAA's) have retained that
- A ship is an "equipment" for the purpose of Clause (iva)
- The Court accepted that the docking station which was leased to Poompuhar, was still at the "disposal" of the non-resident charter-party. That for a moving ship a specific point was not necessary, the docking stating is one where there is commercial and geographic nexus. It thus constitutes a PE
- Though it did not accept the contention that Art. 7 applies. Since while a PE may exist, the income earned by the charter-party was not *attributable to the PE*.
- Since Art. 7 and 8 are not applicable, and since the consideration is in the nature of royalty as per phrase "right to use equipment" Art. 12 of the relevant DTAA must apply. Also so long as royalty does not arise out of a PE it must be treated as royalty under Art. 12, so since it is not attributable to PE it is taxable in resident-state under Art. 12.

## Smit Singapore
- The nature of the agreement was such that entire operation, navigation and management of the vessel shall be in exclusive control and command of the Owners. The Charterers "being only concerned with the result of the services provided".

- There were various material differences between the contract that Poompuhar was dealing with and the time-charter contract in the present case. Since the entire operation, navigation and management of the vessel was in the command of the assessee, Poompuhar was distinguished on facts.
- Relying on Asia Satellite it was held that there is a distinction between letting the asset and the use of the asset for providing services. Therefore, the consideration was not on account of "use" or "right to use" of an "equipment". The equipment was not being used "by" the charterer, but was being used "for the charterer". 
- There was no power to control the vessel and thus the position in Poompuhar could not ipso facto applicable. The amount was received for the services rendered by its vessel along with the crew to Leighton India, and not for allowing the latter to use the equipment. 

## Nan Lian Ship Management
- Relying on Smit Singapore, the Tribunal distinguished Poompuhar on facts, noting that unlike a regular time-charter, the owner did not receive a fixed rate payment. The payment was on the basis of the load of the charterer. The agreement did not envisage the charterer has any control except for carrying the coal as per the requirement of the charterer.

- The hire charges are not independent of the loading capacity and therefore, it cannot be inferred that consideration is payment for hire, rather it is inextricably linked with the carriage of goods.
- The assessee-owner of the ship never separated from the use of the ship in the case of a typical time-charter. Thus the "use" or "right to use" of the "equipment" was never transferred. 

# Art. 7 r/w 5
The taxation of business profits hinges on the existence of PE in the source-state, else the power to tax is apportioned to the residence-state. The primarily question that remains is, if the nature of the agreement does not demonstrate a transfer of the "use" or "right to use" the equipment (LNG Bunkering Barge), **whether the income received from the charter agreement be subject to tax under Art. 7**. 

Paragraph 26 of the OECD MTC Commentary states the following

> 26. **[Ship]** A ship that navigates in international waters or within one or more States is not fixed and does not, therefore, constitute a fixed place of business (unless the operation of the ship is restricted to a particular area that has commercial and geographic coherence). Business activities carried on aboard such a ship, such as the operation of a shop or restaurant, must be treated the same way for the purposes of determining whether paragraph 1 applies (paragraph 5 could apply, however, to some of these activities, e.g. where contracts are concluded when such shops or restaurants are operated within a State).[@oecdModelTaxConvention2017]

A litany of decisions from the Constitutional Courts and the ITAT have deliberated on the issue of what constitutes a PE under India's DTAA's. But two decisions merit consideration in light of the present case. Both discuss the possibility of a ship constituting a PE

## DIT (IT) v. Nederlandsche Overzee Baggermaatsehappiji BV
- The assessee was engaged in business of providing 'dredgers' on charter hire to other parties. It had given a dredger 'Sagar Manthan' to heire on a **bare boat charter basis** to a Dredging and Marine Contractor = HAM

- The AO held that employees of the assessee on board the vessel implied that it constituted a branch office of the assessee and hence a PE of the assessee in India. 
- The ITAT opined that assessee has no personnel located in India for the **purpose for which the contract was entered into with HAM**. The asssessee does not have any responsibility beyond the provision of the dredger on a charter-hire basis. **It is operated by HAM and was used by HAM for the purpose of business**. ==Mere dry lease of an equipment does not constitute a PE==. 

## SeaBird Exploration FZ LLC, In re
- The applicant (non-resident) was involved in rendering **geophysical services** to the ONGC. It had entered into a contract with ONGC to acquire seismic data from the assessee which was to be processed in Bombay High. These services were carried through the assessee's vessel's which were operated in Indian territorial waters for the period under consideration

- It had argued that on this ground, there was no PE of the applicant in India. But the Department contended that the applicant's case was covered by Art. 5(1) of the DTAA.
- The AAR held that, relying on [[Indian SC determines the law on Permanent Establishment|Formula One]] the applicant fulfilled all of the three preconditions for constituting a PE
	- Permanence in terms of duration *to the extant required by the business*
	-  That there was a fixed place in a definite and composite area
	- The fixed place was at the disposal of the applicant
- Thus under article 5(1) of the treaty, the applicant had a fixed place PE in India in the form of its vessels


### Formula One on Disposal

> The principal test, in order to ascertain as to whether an establishment has a fixed place of business or not, is that such physically located premises have to be 'at the disposal’ of the enterprise. For this purpose, it is not necessary that the premises are owned or even rented by the enterprise. It will be sufficient if the premises are put at the disposal of the enterprise. However, merely giving access to such a place to the enterprise for the purposes of the project would not suffice. The place would be treated as at the disposal of the enterprise when the enterprise has right to use the said place and has control thereupon. Taking cue from the word “through” in the article, Vogel has also emphasised that the place of busi- ness qualifies only if the place is at the disposal of the enterprise. According to him, the enterprise will not be able to use the place of business as an instrument for carrying on its business unless it controls the place of business to a considerable extent. [@justicea.ksikriFormulaOneWorld2017a]



### Klaus Vogel on Disposal
- The fact that the tenant/lessee of real property maintains and exercises his business there is not sufficient for a characterization of the property as the business/enterprise of the landlord/lessor. 

- Even if an entrepreneur leases out his whole operable office, an ongoing factory, a workshop, etc., to a new entrepreneur who does not bring any of its own assets or know-how, but only uses the facilities which he has rented, these facilities do not necessarily continue to qualify as the business/enterprise of the owner. 
- Rather, the issue should be decided by careful consideration of all circumstances of the case. Relevant criteria are the assignment of entrepreneurial authority, the allocation of entrepreneurial risks, the actual or potential duration of the leasing relationship, and the criteria on which the remuneration has been based

### Vogel on Fixed Place
- A place is fixed if it shows a link to a specific, distinct geographical point (no. 21 OECD MC Comm. on Article 5) and if this link functions as a connection which avoids any dislocation of the establishment. In this respect, geographical fixation is closely related, but not identical, to the requirement of permanence (see infra m.no. 70 et seq. for details).[@beckerKlausVogelDouble2022, para. 56]

- In contrast, floating facilities at sea have no such link to a geographical point. This is also the case for ships’ berths, and it is certainly the case when a ship is not permanently moored (cf. no. 26 OECD MC Comm. on Article 5). Ships which are permanently moored to the bank (serving as museums, restaurants, hotels, etc.) are, in contrast, ‘fixed’ in the sense of Article 5(1) OECD and UN MC. Similarly, oil rigs firmly anchored to the sea bed are sufficiently linked with a specific geographical point, whereas floating oil rigs which are moved every few months are not. [@beckerKlausVogelDouble2022, para. 58]
