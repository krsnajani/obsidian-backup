<!-- START_ABSTRACT -->
Constitutional patriotism is one of the most attractive yet ill-defined ideas in modern political theory. The idea has become most closely associated with Jürgen Habermas and his attempt to propose a plausible form of collective identification for a postnational Germany and for the European Union.2 But Habermas's explanations have been criticized as too abstract, insufficiently endowed with specific content, and yet, somehow, also too specifically German.3 Though, no doubt, Habermas will continue to be extremely influential in the history of this particular idea, we present here a sustained discussion of alternative views of constitutional patriotism in hopes of both liberating it from Habermas's specific conception and opening up a more general discussion about constitutional patriotism. The extent to which the idea has been embraced, even in the absence of a single coherent account, suggests that many find it useful in various legal and political contexts. We include in this symposium several systematic approaches for thinking about it.
