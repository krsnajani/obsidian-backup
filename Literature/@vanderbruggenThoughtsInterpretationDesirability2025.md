Abstract
This article examines how international investment tribunals have understood and applied exclusions for ‘taxation’ or ‘tax measures’ from the scope of investment treaties and discusses how treaties without a carve-out have been applied to tax-related disputes. The analysis finds that tax carve-outs are a harmful inconsistency in protecting foreign investment and that awards in favour of investors in tax-related claims have concerned State action that was either excessive or entirely avoidable. Adopting tax carve-outs is not aligned with fostering transparency and adherence to international human rights norms but rather undermines the legitimacy of the international tax system.
