The issue is whether the vires of sub-section (4) of the Act, can be challenged in so far as it curtails any possibility of a 'meaningful appeal'. There are two positions that can be formulated on it.

Section 107(4) reads 
>(4) The Appellate Authority may, if he is satisfied that the appellant ==was prevented by sufficient cause== from presenting the appeal within the aforesaid period of three months or six months, as the case may be, ==allow it to be presented within a further period of one month==.

Two positions considering this are:
1. Whether the absence of a equitable remedy of condonation for those having 'sufficient cause' renders this violative of Art. 14, 19 and 21
2. Whether the period specified in the Act is arbitrary.

## Statutory Limits to Condonation of Delay 
The Supreme Court in a litany of its recent judgements has upheld the statutory limits that limit the power of the App. Authority to condone delay. 
### Oil & Natural Gas Corporation Limited v. Gujarat Energy Transmission Corporation Limited
- The question was with respect to delay beyond 120 days in preferring the appeal under Section 125 of the Electricity Act and the question arose whether the delay beyond 120 days in preferring the appeal is condonable or not
- The Court specifically foreclosed the capacity to present an appeal under Art. 142 of the Constitution in case the Tribunal or App. Authority has declined to condone the delay against the statutory prescription.
- When there is a statutory command by the legislation as regards limitation and there is the postulate that delay can be condoned for a period not exceeding 60 days needless to say it is **based on certain underlined fundamental general issues of public policy**. 
- When that is placed, it then comes under Section 3 of the Limitation Act, which states that every application, suit, and appeal filed after the prescribed period shall be dismissed.
- It would come within the ambit and sweep of th[]()e provisions of and policy of legislation and thus it is un-condonable and it cannot be condoned even taking recourse of Art. 142. 
- What cannot be done directly under the statute cannot be done indirectly in exercise of the powers under Art. 142.
### National Spot Exchange Ltd. v. Mr. Anil Kohli RP for Dunar Foods
- The Court was asked to consider the correctness of the App. Tribunal's decision to decline condoning the delay beyond 15 days as prescribed in the statute under Section 61(2) of the IBC.
- The Court held that once a statutory prescription is provided, the Tribunal's compliance with such a mandate cannot be bad in law. 
- While a situation may arise where a applicant may not be e in a position to file an appeal even within a prescribe period under the Act, and then even within the maximum period of appeal, **the Parliament has not carved out any exception of such a situation, and therefore in a given case while it may cause hardship unless the Parliament has carved out any exception by a provision of the law the period of limitation had to be given effect to.**
	- Various cases have been cited by the Court which mention how 'inconvenience is not a decisive factor to be considered when interpreting a statute.'
	- Hardship caused to an individual cannot be a ground for not giving effective and grammatical meaning to each word of the provision.
	- The Court invokes in these cases the maxim ***dura lex, sed lex*** - the law is hard, but it is the law.
	- **Law prevails over equity when there is a conflict, equity can only supplement not supplant the law**. 
### Hari Vishnu Kamath v. Ahmad Ishaque
- This is on the principle that a Court which has jurisdiction over a subject-matter has jurisdiction to decide wrong as well as right, and ==when the Legislature does not choose to confer a right of appeal against that decision, it would be defeating its purpose and policy, if a superior Court were to rehear the case on the evidence== and substitute its own findings in certiorari
- It reinforces the idea that because appeal is a creation of statute specifically when concerned with civil and fiscal statutes, whether the Legislation in its will and wisdom chooses to confer it or not is a question of legislative policy which the Court is not entitled to upset - **in re Delhi Laws Act*** as it a part of the Essential Legislative Function.
### M/s Popular Construction and Co.
- The Court was concerned with the time-limits imposed under Section 34(3) of the Act which prescribes for a 90+30 days for making an application for the setting aside of the arbitral award, and whether in such a prescription condonation could be claimed beyond this. 
- Where the statute has positively prescribed 90 days as time limit for the purpose of filing an application under the Act, the provision is to condone the delay for a further period of 40 days only. 
### R. Gowrishankar v. Commissioner of Service Tax (Appeals)
- As per Section 85 of the Finance Act, 1994, as it existed during the material point of time (prior to 28.05.2012), an appeal ought to have been filed before the Commissioner (Appeals), within three months from the date of communication of the order and the Commissioner (Appeals) is empowered to condone the delay of a further period of three months, if he is satisfied that the appellant was preferred by sufficient cause from presenting the appeal. As it was beyond the condonable period, the appeal was dismissed
- The issue that was raised is because the order that was passed by the *root of the matter* denying a fair representation would not be tenable in law. To dismiss the appeal liminally on the grounds of limitation is invalid, it should have been decided on merits. 
- In rejecting this position, the Court declined to interfere and upset the order made by the Commissioner on the grounds that various decisions of the Court including ***Singh Enterprises v. Com. Central Excise*** have already decided the issue finally. Even if there is a infirmity in the order which goes to the root of the issue, it may not be interfered with. 
### Indian Coffee Worker's Co-op. Society Ltd., v. Commissioner of Commercial Taxes
- The issue was whether the High Court can direct the appellate authority to consider the appeal on merits when the appeal is filed after a 30 day delay from the date on which the appeal should have been filed. 
- When considering the scope of Section 31 of the TN GST Act, the Division Bench of the Mad HC, held under no circumstances the AAA has the power to condone the delay beyond 30 days. While the HC in 226 is concerned with the correctness of the orders of the AA, it has no power to direct the AA to consider the appeals on merits. 
### Thirumalai Chemicals v. Union of India
Right conferred on a party to prefer an appeal against an order is a substantive right conferred by a statute which remains unaffected by subsequent changes in law, unless modified expressly or by necessary implication
