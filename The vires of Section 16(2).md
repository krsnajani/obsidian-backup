## Failure to Pay
It is also essential to note that the second proviso to Section 16(2) mandates reversal of ITC in the event of **failure of payment** to the supplier. 
- The Hon. Allahabad High Court in the case of **_Badri Prasad v. District Judge, Gonda_** (**_1983) All LJ 41 at 42_** held that the parties can be said to have “_failed to pay”_ only if it can be said that they neglected to do something which they were expected to do, or they left some possible or expected action unperformed
- “Failure” means that there is an omission on the part of the person to do something which it is possible for him to do – **High Court of Karnataka** (THATTESSARA SUBBARAYA VERSUS CHINNE GOWDA AND ORS. MYSORE HIGH COURT)
- Failure has been interpreted to mean, “_not doing something that one is expected to do_”. - **_Kavungal Kooppakkattu Zeenath vs. Mundakkattu Sulfiker Ali_** – 2008 (3) TMI 776 – Kerala High Court

Thus the limitation under the Act for the payment of the sale consideration value within 180 days arises after the time contemplated in the contract (which creates a reciprocal duty or expectation) lapses. 

### Similar Provision in CENVAT Act

The CENVAT Act, which is a predecessor to the GST Regime had a similar provision for claiming credit but that time limit began from the date on which the goods have left the premises of the provider of output service.

> The CENVAT credit on inputs shall be allowed even if any inputs as such or after being partially processed are sent to a job worker and from there subsequently sent to another job worker and likewise, for further processing, testing, repairing, re-conditioning or for the manufacture of intermediate goods necessary for the manufacture of final products or any other purpose, and it is established from the records, challans or memos or any other document produced by the manufacturer or the provider of output service taking the CENVAT credit that the inputs or the products produced therefrom are received back by the manufacturer or the provider of output service, as the case may be, ==within one hundred and eighty days of their being sent from the factory or premises of the provider of output service, as the case may be== - CENVAT Credit Rules 2004

Circular No. 122/03/2010- ST dated 30-04-2010. This Circular specifies that when the substantive law i.e. section 67 of the Finance Act treats such book adjustments etc. as deemed payment, there is no reason for denying such extended meaning to the word ‘payment’ for availment of credit. As far as the provisions of Rule 4(7) are concerned, it only provides that the CENVAT credit shall be allowed, on or after the date on which payment is made of the value of the input service and of service tax. The form of payment is not indicated in the same and the rule does not place restriction on payment through debit in the books of accounts.

### Meeting 5
- The question was raised in respect of second proviso in as to whether tax would be payable in a situation where **a contract between two taxable persons could provide for a period of making payment beyond three months**
- The Commissioner stated that the limitation of three months was an *anti-evasion* measure and that credit reversed after three months could be against taken once the recipient of service had made a payment to the supplier
- The Council after further discussion, agreed to keep similar provision for goods and services and further agreed that the time period for making payments shall be increased from three months to six months from the date of issuance of invoice.
### Meeting 6
- The issue was taken up with respect to their applicability on services. It was argued that the law prescribed a distinction for services because unlike goods, there could be a presumption of fake billing as there is no method to check the supply of services. 
- The Commissioner, Commercial Taxes also raised the issue that at times quality testing etc. on goods could take longer than three months, and payment could be delayed on that account too.
- Finally the Council agreed to keep the similar provision for goods and services and agreed that the time period for making payment shall be increased from three months to six months from the date of issuance of invoice
### Meeting 28

#### Definition of MSME and Contemporaneous Exposition
- Contemporanea expositio is a legal doctrine asserting that the most accurate interpretation of a statute or document is the one understood by its original creators or established through long-standing professional usage. This contemporary or historical understanding is presumed to be correct, even if the language might otherwise suggest a different meaning today. It has been consistently followed by the Supreme Court, especially in tax cases. 
- At the time when the issue was being debated by the GST Council, the definition of MSME's as per Section 7 of the MSME Act was as follows

| Classification | Manufacturing Enterprises (Investment in Plant & Machinery) | Service Enterprises (Investment in Equipment) |
| -------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Micro**      | Up to **₹25 Lakh**                                          | Up to **₹10 Lakh**                            |
| **Small**      | Above ₹25 Lakh to **₹5 Crore**                              | Above ₹10 Lakh to **₹2 Crore**                |
| **Medium**     | Above ₹5 Crore to **₹10 Crore**                             | Above ₹2 Crore to **₹5 Crore**                |
This is before an expansive definition was given to MSME's and thus at the time it was being debated the word MSME's was constrained to those with 10 lakh - 5 crore in investments. Today as per the revision made in 2025, it stands at the following values. 

| Classification | Investment (Plant & Machinery/Equipment) | Annual Turnover      |
| -------------- | ---------------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| **Micro**      | Up to **₹2.5 Crore**                     | Up to **₹10 Crore**  |
| **Small**      | Up to **₹25 Crore**                      | Up to **₹10 Crore**  |
| **Medium**     | Up to **₹125 Crore**                     | Up to **₹500 Crore** |
Not only have the values changed, but also the mechanism for calculating these values has changed, with the introduction of the total turnover mechanism. This means that ==the understanding of MSME's shared by the GST Council at the time when it was discussing the implications of deleting the interest provision to the Second Proviso was the **old and limited definition of an MSME**==

The principle that can be discerned form this is thus = An MSME in the understanding of the Council was one that was essentially a small enterprise with limited investment in equipment, plant or machinery. This meant that the statute was never **intended to be applicable on all industries.**
