Every supply must involve either goods or services, or a composition of goods or services, or both. 

If the supply involves multiple goods or services, which are naturally bundled with each other, and supplied in conjunction in the **ordinary course of business** and one of the supply is a **principal** then it constitutes a **composite supply*. 
- Taxable Person - Not only those who have registered, but also those liable to be registered.
- Taxable Supplies - Both supply should fall within the ambit of taxable supply. 
- Naturally Bundled - It be taken on a fact-to-fact basis. 
- Ordinary Course of Business - It is essential that they are supplied in the ordinary course, of that supplier's business. For instance, installation with water purifier is a combination that is naturally bundled but if it is not actually **ordinarily provided** in the course of his business, then it is not a composite supply. 
- Principal Supply - There must always be one principal supply, which is the predominant element of the supply to which other articles are ancillary. 
The various details including time of supply, invoicing, place of suply etc shall all be determined in respect of the principal supply alone, because the effect of composite supply is that the entire supply is the **supply of the principal supply alone.** 

On the contrary, if there is some supply of more than one goods and/or services which are **not naturally bundled together** but sold for a single price then it is a **mixed supply**.
- Individual Supply - Unlike composite supply which involves only taxable supply, mixed supply may also include those that are not taxable (exempt supply)
- Taxable Person 
- Single Price - That a composite supply does not include this condition, is to be noted. Where supply of goods is made for different prices, the supply cannot be considered as mixed supply
	- Thus, Buy One Get One Free constitutes as a possible example of mixed supply because it may be difficult to identify a principal supply. It may be argued that two goods are supplied for a the price of one (single price). 
Canned foods, sweets and chocolate cakes are examples of composite supply, where all of these items could be supplied separately and are not dependent on each other, **plus** they are taxed at a single price. This implies that the supply will be taxed as a mixed supply. Further, **they will be liable to the highest rate of taxation possible on any of the constituting supply.**
- Toothpaste = 12% is bundled with a Toothpaste = 18% then GST on a mixed supply of both for a single price will be = 18%. 

The AAR ,West Bengal opined in _Switching_ _Avo Electro Power Ltd. [2018 (13) G.S.T.L. 84 (A.A.R. - GST)]_.-_“ When a combination of goods that does not amount to a composite supply is being offered at a single price, such supplies are to be treated as mixed supplies"
