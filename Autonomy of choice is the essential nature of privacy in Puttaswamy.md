A major section of [[@chandrachudJusticePuttaswamyAnr2017|Puttaswamy]] is concerned with developing the constitutional rationale required to justify a right to privacy being read into Art. 22. The Court was called upon to settle the position of law after divergent decisions had been given in a variety of cases, where privacy was read into the Constitution on an erroneous reading of existing precedent. But since then, reading ***Kharak Singh*** and others, the Court affirmed privacy as an inalienable right primarily read through Art. 21 but also implicit largely in Part III.

Chapter **R** [@chandrachudJusticePuttaswamyAnr2017, pp. 242-246] marks the discussion on the *nature of privacy*. These are the key points:

1. Privacy postulates the reservation of *private spaces* for the individual - ***right to be left alone***. This concept is founded on the *autonomy of the individual.* 
2. This ability take decisions regarding the matters of human life, is also an essential part of personality. This is essential the right to reserve physical and mental spaces for the development of one's personality. The recognition of privacy acknowledges that persons can chart the course of their own *personality*. This is why privacy is also a *postulate of **dignity***
3. It is only in privacy can one truly without coercion determine what one wishes to do with liberty. It is thus an essential precondition of liberty. Freedom (Liberty) and Dignity are also inter-twined *each being a facilitative tool to achieve the other* [@chandrachudJusticePuttaswamyAnr2017, p. 243]. The ability to reserve a space for oneself means that expression, when read with privacy also extends protection to *silence*
4. Both dignity and privacy exist in the realm of **constitutional values**, which are not clearly enumerated but "*reside within the inalienable values rights of life, liberty and freedom which the Constitution has recognised*". [@chandrachudJusticePuttaswamyAnr2017, p. 245]
